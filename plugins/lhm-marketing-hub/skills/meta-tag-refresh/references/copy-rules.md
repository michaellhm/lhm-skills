# Copy Rules — Titles and Meta Descriptions

## Length

- Titles: aim for 50-60 characters. Up to ~63 is acceptable if the keyword payload is front-loaded so truncation only costs the brand suffix. Google truncates around 600px, roughly 60 characters.
- Descriptions: 120-160 characters. Under 120 wastes SERP real estate; over 160 gets cut mid-sentence.
- Put live LEN() formulas in the spreadsheet so edits keep the counts honest.

## Title construction

- Lead with the keyword phrasing that converts, not the one with the most volume. Check the converting search terms first: if "dry eye clinic kotara" converts and "dry eye treatment kotara" doesn't, the title leads with "Clinic". This clinic-vs-treatment pattern generalises: match the noun people transact with (clinic, dentist, lawyer, course) over the abstract service noun when conversion data shows the preference.
- Location pages: `[Service] Clinic [Suburb] | [secondary phrasing]` — cover both the "clinic" and "treatment/service" variants in one title where characters allow. Add a regional geo-modifier (North Shore, Central Coast, Inner West) in title or description: it widens the local net and disambiguates suburbs that exist in multiple states.
- Homepage: keyword-led with brand suffix, unless brand queries dominate conversions AND the brand SERP needs strengthening — then brand can lead. Use city names people search ("Sydney"), not administrative regions ("NSW").
- Nearby converting suburbs with no page of their own (e.g. searches from an adjacent suburb converting into an existing clinic) get mentioned in that page's description, and a dedicated page recommendation goes in the launch checklist.
- Match search phrasing exactly where the data shows it ("Costs" if people search cost queries that rank).

## Description construction

Descriptions are ads. Build them from angles with proven CTR in the client's own ad account:
1. Pull the RSA descriptions and ad-level CTR data (Phase 2). Identify the top angles: offers (free consult/telehealth call), friction removers (no referral needed), authority claims (named protocols, training, clinic count), and emotional hooks ("tried drops without lasting relief").
2. Lead each description with the strongest relevant angle for that page, end with a soft CTA (Book online today / Book online, no referral needed).
3. Vary the constructions across pages — 18 descriptions that all read "X. Y. Book online." look templated to users and to Google. Rotate sentence patterns.
4. Include the phone number in the contact page description if call conversions are material in the account.

## Compliance — health clients (AHPRA)

These rules were established in a real compliance pass and are not optional for AU health clients:

- Never use "specialist" or "specialise" for practitioners who don't hold an endorsed specialist title (optometrists, physios, chiros, podiatrists cannot). This applies to titles and descriptions even when "specialist" is the converting keyword — target the keyword with AHPRA-safe variants: clinic, dedicated, [profession] (e.g. "dry eye optometrist"), advanced care. Keyword targeting in ads/ad-group names is fine; on-page claims are not.
- No outcome guarantees, no superlatives you can't substantiate. "Australia's only X" needs proof; "Australia's leading X" is defensible phrasing — but check the client has signed off on the claim previously (look in prior reports) before reusing.
- Factual authority claims beat protected titles: named training ("Toyos-trained"), named protocols, clinic counts, mentorship arrangements — verify they're documented in the client folder before using.
- No testimonial language in metas.

## Formatting

- No em dashes anywhere in titles or descriptions. Use pipes, commas, or full stops.
- Sentence-natural capitalisation in descriptions; Title Case is fine in titles.
- Exact brand spelling in titles. If GSC shows a plural/misspelt brand variant with real volume ranking poorly, note it in the deliverable rather than corrupting the brand string.

## Rationale column

Every row's rationale must cite data, not taste: "GSC: 1,036 imps at pos 14.6" or "converted 4 bookings in 12m" or "AHPRA: removed specialist claim". If the user later questions a title, the spreadsheet should answer without re-running the research.
